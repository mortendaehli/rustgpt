The lighthouse watched the harbor every night, and the harbor answered with a line of silver waves.
On windy evenings the keeper wrote short notes about fog, gulls, and the ships that arrived late.
When the storm passed, the town woke to bright roofs, wet cobblestones, and a sky that looked newly made.
The baker opened early, the bell rang twice, and the street filled with warm bread and impatient footsteps.
At the edge of town a narrow bridge crossed a cold stream and led into a pine forest that smelled of rain.
Children said the forest kept secrets. The old gardener said the forest simply listened better than most people.
Every morning he carried a tin watering can, trimmed the roses, and spoke to the soil as if it were an old friend.
By autumn the market square turned amber. Crates of pears and apples stood beside jars of honey and folded wool blankets.
At dusk the lanterns came on one by one, and the windows of the houses shone like quiet promises.
No one in the town hurried without reason. Even the trains seemed to arrive with a measured kind of patience.
